01 — Demand

The audience

Before broadcasters caught up, fans were already watching. YouTube search data across three major women's leagues tells the story of an organic surge in demand. Measured not in what leagues posted, but in what the entire internet watched.

Each data point represents the average views of the 35 most-watched videos for that league in that year, sourced from across all of YouTube.

The Women's World Cup effect

France 2019 drove a record spike in women's football viewership globally, pulling WSL search traffic up with it, even in the off-season.

Pandemic rebound + streaming shift

As live sport returned post-lockdown, audiences used to watching on screens didn't go back to TV-only habits. YouTube views for all three leagues accelerated.

PWHL launches

The Professional Women's Hockey League launched in January 2024 and immediately generated YouTube traffic that took the WNBA years to build.

Caitlin Clark changes everything

The WNBA's 2024 draft class (led by Caitlin Clark) drove the league's highest-ever YouTube engagement, with individual highlight videos crossing 2M views.

02 — Broadcast Time

On screen, the gap
remains stark.

Despite the growth in digital audiences, traditional broadcast coverage of women's sport has barely shifted in three decades. In 2019, women's sport received just 5.4% of US sports TV airtime — almost identical to the 5% recorded in 1989. About this project

This visual essay was built to track the growth in women's sports viewership and media visibility using publicly available data from the YouTube Data API v3, the Women's Sport Trust annual Visibility Reports, and published Nielsen Sports research.

YouTube ecosystem data was collected by searching for the top 35 most-viewed videos per league per year across the entire platform — capturing fan uploads, broadcaster clips, and viral moments, not just official league channels.
